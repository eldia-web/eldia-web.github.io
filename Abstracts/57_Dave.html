Learning the Scientific Method through an Evolving Hydrologic Experiment

Presenter: Dave Wessner1
Co-Author(s): -
Advisor(s): Dr. Ty Ferre

1Department of Hydrology and Atmospheric Sciences

Panapto Presentation Video
Poster Session 1

The importance of the scientific method in experiment design is often overlooked in studies. Many opt for a single hypothesis with results in place of a story that depicts the evolution of their hypothesis and experiment structure as the study progressed. In my study I have attempted to remove this separation of results from the experiment development and evolution process. The paper consists of two parts; the first is a scientific investigation into the value that different datatypes provide in the constraint of unsaturated flow parameters in van Genuchten soil parameter predictions via ensemble generation, and the second is a conscious examination of the learning process through the experiment’s evolution. The scientific investigation consisted of sequentially calibrating HYDRUS-1D models with volumetric water content data recorded in-situ to describe infiltration and drainage of the subsurface, beginning with a homogenous 1-dimensional model based upon visual observations of the test-pit’s soil composition. These initial results led to modifications of the model structure over time, with additions such as layering of different soil-types within the system, the expansion of the parameter space used in generating the models by predicting soil parameters from grain-size composition data, and the addition of temperature data at several depths during infiltration and drainage events to elicit a model response similar to the field data. The second part of my study is an introspective look into how my understanding of hydrologic processes grew, adapted, and influenced each incremental step of the scientific process over the course of the experiment.
